## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) was introduced by [Lewis et al., 2020] as a framework for grounding language model outputs in retrieved documents via vector similarity search. While effective for single-hop factual queries, standard RAG suffers from context fragmentation at chunk boundaries and lacks the ability to reason across multiple retrieval steps. Subsequent graph-based approaches have sought to address these limitations. GraphRAG [Edge et al., 2024, arXiv:2404.16130] constructs community-level summaries over entity graphs to support both global and local search. LightRAG [Guo et al., 2024, arXiv:2410.05779] performs free-form relation extraction followed by dual-level graph retrieval; the relation extraction strategy in Stage 1 of our two-tier edge inference is conceptually inspired by this approach. RAPTOR [Sarthi et al., 2024, arXiv:2401.18059] builds recursive abstractive trees over documents, sharing structural similarities with the PageIndex tree used in this work, though it requires offline pre-indexing and does not support dynamic traversal. HippoRAG [Gutierrez et al., 2024, arXiv:2405.14831] draws on hippocampal memory models, employing Personalized PageRank over a knowledge graph to support associative multi-hop retrieval.

### 2.2 Agentic Information Retrieval

There is growing interest in positioning LLMs as active retrieval agents rather than passive consumers of retrieved content. Self-RAG [Asai et al., 2024, ICLR 2024 Oral] inserts reflection tokens such as `[Retrieve]` and `[IsSUP]` during generation to make token-level decisions about retrieval necessity, realizing adaptive retrieval within a single decoding pass without explicit planning or state management. PRISM [Nahid & Rafiei, 2025, arXiv:2510.14278] decomposes retrieval into a three-agent architecture (Analyzer, Selector, Adder) that separates precision and recall objectives; its convergence-based stopping criterion is conceptually analogous to the dynamic termination implemented in our `plan_next_search()` function, and achieves 90.9% recall on HotpotQA. Search-o1 [Li et al., 2025, EMNLP 2025, arXiv:2501.05366] detects knowledge-uncertain points within a reasoning chain and dynamically triggers web search, distilling retrieved results through a Reason-in-Documents module before reinjecting them into the reasoning trace — operating within a single reasoning pass, in contrast to our multi-hop State-Action-Transition loop. APEX-Searcher [Chen et al., 2026, arXiv:2603.13853] combines reinforcement learning with supervised fine-tuning, separating a planning stage (RL with decomposition rewards) from an execution stage (SFT over multi-step trajectories) to avoid sparse reward problems; while most directly comparable to our approach in terms of task scope, it requires training. Game of Thought [Cui et al., 2026, LM4Plan @ ICAPS 2025, arXiv:2602.01708], a concurrent workshop paper accepted at the same venue (LM4Plan), models information seeking as a two-player zero-sum game under incomplete information and derives worst-case optimal query policies via Nash equilibrium strategies.

A parallel line of work explores documents as navigable structures rather than flat retrieval targets. ReadAgent [Lee et al., 2024, ICML 2024, arXiv:2402.09727] compresses document pages into gist memories and selectively looks up originals when needed, but operates over flat page sequences without hierarchical structure or persistent state. DocAgent [Sun et al., 2025, EMNLP 2025] extracts a tree-formatted XML outline from documents and uses an actor-reviewer agent pair to navigate it; however, the agent does not maintain a knowledge graph as planning state and lacks dynamic termination based on evidence sufficiency. BookRAG [Wang et al., 2024, arXiv:2512.03413] constructs a hierarchical index (table of contents + entity graph) and routes queries to tailored retrieval workflows, but functions as a query classifier and dispatcher rather than a multi-step planning agent. PageIndex [Zhang & Tang, 2025] builds a vectorless document tree and uses LLM reasoning for top-down traversal; critically, it operates as a **single-pass retrieval tool** — the tree is fed into LLM prompts to select branches, not exposed as a formal environment with which an agent interacts through actions and state updates.

The present work departs from these approaches by constructing the document tree as a **scalable, text-based environment** that an LLM agent interacts with through defined actions (browse, read, search), while maintaining a persistent knowledge graph as planning state and applying closed-loop online planning with dynamic termination. This environment-centric formulation — rather than prompt-based tree traversal or query routing — enables the agent to perform multi-hop exploration with state-conditioned replanning at each hop. The approach is further distinguished by its domain-specific regulatory edge ontology (SATISFIES/VIOLATES), vision-augmented multimodal processing, and training-free design.

### 2.3 LLM-based Planning & Agent Frameworks

A substantial body of work explores the use of LLMs as planning and reasoning engines. ReAct [Yao et al., 2023] interleaves reasoning traces with action execution, a paradigm that is conceptually aligned with the planning loop in our agent. Tree-of-Thought [Yao et al., 2024] extends this by organizing deliberation over a search tree, bearing structural similarity to our document tree traversal. Toolformer [Schick et al., 2024] enables LLMs to autonomously invoke external tools, paralleling our agent's selection among browse, read, and search actions. Notably, much of the existing LLM planning literature focuses on PDDL, robotic, or web environments; by contrast, this work frames the planning problem within an **information environment** composed of regulatory documents.

Regarding world models and state representation, Ha & Schmidhuber [2018] demonstrated that agents can maintain internal representations of their environment to inform action planning. More recently, GWM [Feng et al., 2025, ICML 2025] adopts graph-structured state representations with message-passing-based action selection. Our work similarly employs a Dynamic Sub-KG as an internal planning state, but departs from embedding-based approaches by generating explicit relational edges (SATISFIES, VIOLATES) through LLM inference rather than vector representations — a training-free alternative that also enables interpretable compliance verification. This two-tier edge inference additionally aligns with the broader theme of using LLMs for plan and policy verification, where the LLM judges whether collected evidence satisfies regulatory requirements.

### 2.4 Knowledge Graph for RAG

Knowledge graph-augmented RAG methods leverage explicit triples to strengthen multi-hop reasoning [Pan et al., 2024]. A growing subset of this literature explores dynamic KG construction, where query-specific graphs are built at inference time, eliminating the cost of pre-constructing a static KG; our Dynamic Sub-KG falls within this category. The specific edge relations employed in this work draw on several established precedents: SATISFIES and REFINES originate from systems engineering and SysML requirements traceability [Friedenthal et al., 2014]; the regulatory compliance domain has prior ontological treatment in construction regulation checking [Zhong et al., 2012]; SUPPORTS and CONTRADICTS derive from argumentation mining [Peldszus & Stede, 2013] and have been applied to online debate analysis [Cabrio & Villata, 2012, ACL]; IS_PREREQUISITE_OF is grounded in prerequisite relation learning [Pan et al., 2017, ACL]; and LEADS_TO is motivated by causal knowledge graph construction [Hassanzadeh et al., 2019, IJCAI].

### 2.5 Multimodal RAG

Existing multimodal document understanding benchmarks such as DocVQA and ChartQA address single-image question answering in isolation. The present work extends this setting to multi-hop retrieval with interleaved multimodal reasoning, requiring the agent to first identify relevant figures and tables through tree traversal before applying vision-based answer generation.

### 2.6 Nuclear Domain NLP

Prior NLP work targeting the nuclear domain includes NuclearQA [Acharya et al., 2023, arXiv:2310.10920], a benchmark for evaluating language models on nuclear science texts, and NukeBERT [Jain et al., 2020, arXiv:2003.13821], a domain-adapted pre-trained language model. NuclearQA focuses primarily on single-hop factual extraction and does not support evaluation of multi-hop regulatory judgment, which constitutes a primary motivation for the benchmark design presented in this paper.

### 2.7 RAG Evaluation

We adopt two established evaluation protocols. RAGAS [Es et al., 2024, arXiv:2309.15217, EACL 2024] provides an automated framework for RAG assessment across four metrics: Faithfulness, Answer Relevancy, Context Recall, and Factual Correctness. For qualitative judgment, we employ the LLM-as-Judge paradigm [Zheng et al., 2023] using GPT-4 with a three-evaluator majority vote to ensure robustness of the automated scoring.
